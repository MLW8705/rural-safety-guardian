Build Your Emergency Kits

Use the same collapsible checklist style to review what each kit should cover.

Use a grab-and-go bag for fast evacuation, overnight sheltering, or sudden road closures that keep you away from home.

  • Essentials: Water, shelf-stable food, flashlight, batteries, phone charger, small first-aid kit, and copies of important contacts.
  • Clothing: Sturdy shoes, socks, rain layer, work gloves, seasonal layers, hat, and a compact blanket.
  • Family items: Medications, baby supplies, comfort items, hygiene basics, eyeglasses, and backup power for medical devices.
  • Rural-specific additions: Paper map, gate combinations, livestock notes, spare truck key, and a weather radio if cell coverage is weak.

This kit supports the household when roads are blocked, power is out, or weather makes travel unsafe.

  • Water storage: Store drinking water plus extra for cooking, pets, and sanitation needs.
  • Food: Keep easy meals that do not depend on refrigeration or long cook times.
  • Lighting and heat: Flashlights, lanterns, safe backup heat plans, extra fuel where appropriate, and plenty of batteries.
  • Hygiene: Trash bags, wipes, toilet paper, basic cleaning supplies, and a simple handwashing backup.
  • Communication: Battery radio, charging banks, printed contact lists, and a plan for spotty service.
  • Rural-specific items: Well-water backup, safe generator setup, freezer and fridge monitoring, and feed or bedding reserves for animals.

Road delays hit harder in rural areas, so your vehicle kit should handle breakdowns, weather, and long waits for help.

  • Winter items: Warm gloves, hats, blanket, traction aid, scraper, and spare dry layers.
  • Breakdown essentials: Jumper cables, flashlight, tire tools, reflective triangles, and a power bank.
  • Rural-specific tools: Tow strap, shovel, fence pliers, work gloves, and extra fluids for long gravel routes.
  • Safety gear: Water, snacks, first-aid kit, visibility vest, and printed directions if the signal drops.
