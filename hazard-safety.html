Weather and Hazard Safety

Open the topic you need instead of scanning one long page during a stressful moment.

  • Move inside before thunder and strong wind arrive, not after damage starts.
  • Stay away from windows during hail, flying debris, and lightning-producing storms.
  • Do not walk under trees or near metal gates during lightning danger.
  • Watch for blocked or washed-out access on long driveways after the storm.
  • Shelter indoors away from windows and avoid rooms with large unsupported roofs if possible.
  • Secure loose items early, including trailers, feed tubs, tarps, and lightweight outbuilding materials.
  • Delay driving high-profile vehicles when gusts become hard to control.
  • Stay far from downed lines, damaged barns, and leaning poles after wind events.
  • Use the lowest, most interior shelter space available and protect your head.
  • Do not wait to visually confirm a tornado before taking shelter.
  • Mobile homes and weak outbuildings are not safe tornado shelter.
  • Because rural warnings can arrive with limited lead time, keep shelter access fast and uncluttered.
  • Leave early if fire weather worsens, especially when there is only one reliable route out.
  • Keep defensible space around buildings and move combustibles away from siding and porches.
  • Prepare transport plans for pets and livestock before smoke or flames are nearby.
  • Wear sturdy clothing and avoid breathing heavy smoke if you must work outside briefly.
  • Stock extra heat, lighting, food, and water before roads drift shut.
  • Limit travel in whiteout conditions and keep vehicles winter-ready in advance.
  • Protect pipes, pumps, and animal water systems from freezing.
  • Watch for roof loads, barn ventilation problems, and extended isolation from help.
  • Use generators outdoors only and keep fuel handled safely.
  • Plan for well-water interruption if your pump depends on electricity.
  • Preserve cold food with minimal fridge opening and use thermometers if available.
  • Keep lanterns, flashlights, and backup charging ready before the outage starts.
  • Long response times mean you should report hazards early and plan to be self-sufficient longer.
  • Gate access, bridge limits, and muddy roads can slow emergency vehicles.
  • Outbuildings, propane systems, wells, and animal areas add extra safety checks after storms.
  • Keep neighbors informed because informal local networks often matter before formal help arrives.
