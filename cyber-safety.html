Digital Safety for Everyday Life

Protect your accounts, devices, and family from common online risks.

This page gathers plain-language guidance for safer passwords, scam awareness, device setup, and quick-reference materials that can be printed for home use.

Cyber Readiness

Focus Areas Accounts, devices, and scams

Good cyber safety habits do not need to be complicated. A few consistent steps make most families much harder to target.

Cyber Safety Section

Review practical habits, common scams, and printable reminders for the whole household.

  • Password safety: Use long, unique passwords for important accounts, especially email, banking, and school logins.
  • MFA: Turn on multi-factor authentication so a stolen password alone is not enough to get in.
  • Software updates: Keep phones, laptops, routers, apps, and browsers updated so known security issues get patched.
  • Phishing awareness: Slow down before clicking urgent links, unexpected invoices, or account warning messages.
  • Safe downloads: Download apps and files only from trusted official sources, not random links in messages or ads.
  • Device security: Use screen locks, automatic updates, and device tracking or recovery settings when available.
  • Wi-Fi and router safety: Change default router passwords, use WPA2/WPA3 security, and keep guest access separate when possible.
Utility Shutoff Scams

Scammers demand instant payment and threaten disconnection. Always contact the utility using the number on your real bill.

Livestock Sale Scams

Fake buyers or sellers may push fast deposits, unusual payment apps, or suspicious shipping promises.

Facebook Marketplace Scams

Be cautious with overpayments, verification code requests, and buyers who refuse normal in-person safety steps.

Fake Neighbor Messages

Messages may pretend to come from someone local asking for money, codes, or emergency help without proof.

Farm Equipment Scams

Listings with prices too good to be true, vague locations, or pressure to pay before inspection are major warning signs.

FEMA or Disaster Scams

After storms, scammers may pose as aid workers or inspectors. Verify identity before sharing money or personal details.

Romance Scams

Scammers build trust over time and then ask for money, gift cards, or account help while avoiding real-world verification.

  • Laptops: Keep antivirus and system updates on, use separate accounts when possible, and encrypt the device if supported.
  • Tablets: Apply the same basics as phones: lock code, app review, updates, and restricted install sources.
  • Kids' devices: Review app permissions, privacy settings, spending controls, and content restrictions before regular use.
  • Strong passwords: Aim for long passphrases or randomly generated passwords instead of short reused ones.
  • Safe storage: Use a trusted password manager or another secure method rather than reusing the same easy password everywhere.
  • MFA importance: Add multi-factor authentication to email, bank, school, health, and social media accounts.
  • Breach checking: If an account may be exposed, change the password immediately and review recent account activity.
  • Social media safety: Keep profiles private, avoid oversharing location details, and think before posting photos or personal information.
  • Cyberbullying signs: Watch for sudden withdrawal, stress around devices, deleted accounts, or fear after messages arrive.
  • Safe gaming: Be careful with voice chat, direct messages, fake in-game offers, and pressure to click links or send codes.
  • Privacy settings: Review them together regularly because apps and platforms change over time.
  • When to involve school or authorities: Report threats, stalking, extortion, sexual exploitation, or persistent harassment right away.

Card 1

Password & Account Safety

  • Use long, unique passwords for email, banking, and school logins.
  • Turn on MFA anywhere it is offered, especially for email and financial accounts.
  • Store passwords in a trusted manager or another secure method, not in reused notes.
  • Change passwords quickly if an account may be exposed or a device is lost.

Card 2

Phishing & Scam Spotting

  • Slow down when a message sounds urgent, threatening, or too good to be true.
  • Check the sender, the link address, and any phone number before responding.
  • Never share passwords, security codes, or gift card numbers through messages.
  • Use official account numbers or known websites instead of message links.

Card 3

Safe Device Setup

  • Use a screen lock, current software updates, and automatic backups on every device.
  • Turn on device location or recovery settings before an emergency or theft happens.
  • Review app permissions and remove apps you do not trust or use anymore.
  • Secure home Wi-Fi with a strong router password and modern encryption.

Card 4

Kids & Teens Online Safety

  • Keep profiles private and avoid sharing live location or school schedules publicly.
  • Talk early about stranger contact, scams in games, and pressure to send photos or codes.
  • Watch for signs of cyberbullying, isolation, or fear around messages and apps.
  • Report threats, extortion, or exploitation right away to school staff or authorities.
