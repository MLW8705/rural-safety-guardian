Emergency Planning

Review the household decisions that make the rest of your supplies and tools easier to use.

Communication plans matter more in rural areas where coverage can drop quickly after storms or during power outages.

  • Choose one local meeting point and one backup location farther away.
  • Pick an out-of-area contact who can relay updates if local lines are jammed.
  • Print phone numbers and addresses so they remain available when devices fail.
  • Teach everyone how to send a short check-in message with location and next step.
  • Know where to go for tornado shelter, wildfire evacuation, and winter road closures.
  • Set early triggers for leaving when long driveways or muddy roads can become impassable.
  • Plan who drives which vehicle and who handles pets, livestock paperwork, or medications.
  • Keep keys, fuel habits, and loading priorities consistent so leaving is less chaotic.
  • Store supplies in known spots and label backup flashlights, radios, and chargers clearly.
  • Check smoke alarms, carbon monoxide alarms, extinguishers, and generator safety supplies.
  • Rotate food, water, medications, and animal feed on a simple seasonal schedule.
  • Keep fridge and freezer safety plans ready for extended outages.
  • Keep halters, carriers, feed, medications, and transport options ready before an emergency starts.
  • Write down where animals can shelter and who can help if you are away or injured.
  • Store vet contacts, tag numbers, and photos for quicker identification if animals get loose.
  • Decide early which animals can evacuate and which may need sheltering in place.
  • Know which neighbors may need extra help and which ones have equipment, chainsaws, or generators.
  • Write down gate access, alternate routes, and who can check on the property if you leave.
  • Keep insurance contacts, photos of property, and a short damage documentation checklist ready.
  • Review the plan twice a year so it stays realistic as roads, animals, or household needs change.
